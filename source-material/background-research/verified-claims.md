## Regulation — NYDFS / GLBA / MFA mandates

- MFA is a direct regulatory driver for carrier-portal access: NY DFS 23 NYCRR Part 500, with an update effective Nov. 1, 2025, requires multi-factor authentication for any individual accessing covered entities' information systems, and other states tend to follow New York's lead. — *(supporting / secondary / 2026-02-12)*
- Credential-using aggregators are not subject to the same regulatory supervision as the regulated institutions whose portals they access, and policy bodies push to extend GLBA-style standards (Regulation P, Interagency Information Security Guidelines) to them. — *(supporting / primary / 2020-02-20)*

## Legal — CFAA / scraping precedent

- Violating a public website's Terms of Service / user agreement alone does not trigger CFAA liability, and the CFAA's 'without authorization' provision does not apply to public websites; the Ninth Circuit is therefore the most pro-scraping circuit. — *(central / secondary / 2022-12-22)*
- Even where CFAA does not apply, website operators retain enforceable remedies against scrapers via breach-of-contract claims; provisions of a website user agreement prohibiting data scraping and fake profiles are enforceable under a breach of contract claim. — *(central / secondary / 2022-12-22)*
- The LinkedIn v. hiQ litigation ended in a confidential settlement (stipulation filed December 6, 2022) with a $500,000 judgment against hiQ, liability under California common law torts of trespass to chattels and misappropriation, and an injunction barring hiQ from future scraping. — *(supporting / secondary / 2022-12-22)*
- Common law tort claims (e.g., trespass to chattels, misappropriation) in addition to breach of contract are available remedies against unwanted data scrapers, meaning automated access can incur liability even on public sites. — *(central / secondary / 2022-12-22)*
- The settlement stipulation in LinkedIn v. hiQ has no precedential value because it is not a court finding of fact. — *(tangential / secondary / 2022-12-22)*
- While the Ninth Circuit found scraping publicly available data may be lawful under the CFAA, the hiQ v. LinkedIn settlement still held hiQ liable for accessing password-protected pages using fake accounts, and the permanent injunction made no distinction between public and password-protected portions of the site. — *(central / secondary / 2022-12-08)*
- hiQ was found to have breached LinkedIn's User Agreement (Terms of Service) through its scraping and creation of fake accounts, showing that contract/ToS-based claims provide a liability path independent of the CFAA. — *(central / secondary / 2022-12-08)*
- hiQ stipulated to liability for trespass to chattels and misappropriation, and under California state law for unauthorized commercial use of data and evasion tactics, demonstrating state-law theories remain viable even where the CFAA does not apply. — *(supporting / secondary / 2022-12-08)*
- The hiQ v. LinkedIn settlement set no binding precedent, leaving the legality of automated/scraped access to websites legally uncertain. — *(supporting / secondary / 2022-12-08)*
- The case resulted in a $500,000 judgment against hiQ and a permanent injunction barring it from accessing LinkedIn in violation of the User Agreement without express written permission. — *(tangential / secondary / 2022-12-08)*
- In Van Buren v. United States (decided June 3, 2021), the Supreme Court adopted a narrow reading of the CFAA's 'exceeds authorized access' provision, holding 6-3 that the statute does not reach individuals with improper motives who obtain information otherwise available to them. — *(central / secondary / 2021-06-06)*

## Risk — banking aggregator precedent

- Carrier portal login processes are inconsistent across carriers, with at least three distinct login types (SSO, no-login bridging, and separate username/password) coexisting even within a single aggregator platform spanning ~30 carriers. — *(central / primary / 2025 (footer copyright only; no specific publish/last-updated date))*
- Carrier credentials are distributed through fragmented channels (directly by the carrier or via email from the aggregator), and lost credentials require searching inboxes or contacting the carrier/aggregator support team. — *(supporting / primary / 2025 (footer copyright only; no specific publish/last-updated date))*
- There is unresolved liability ambiguity over who bears responsibility for unauthorized transactions and cyber breaches when a third-party aggregator uses stored credentials, with the credential-holding institution arguing it should not be liable for aggregator-caused data loss. — *(central / primary / 2020-02-20)*
- Major banks (Wells Fargo, PNC) are actively demanding that third-party data aggregators stop screen-scraping customer data and switch to sanctioned APIs, demonstrating that institutions push back against automated credential-based portal access in the financial sector. — *(central / secondary / 2025-11-07)*
- Aggregators have historically operated by collecting customer usernames and passwords and then impersonating the customer to log into the bank's portal with those credentials, the exact credential-vault-plus-automated-login model that institutions object to. — *(central / secondary / 2025-11-07)*
- Institutions characterize screen scraping as actively harmful to security and fraud detection, citing blocked servers and interference with fraud systems, an operational/relationship risk for credential-replay aggregators. — *(supporting / secondary / 2025-11-07)*
- Aggregators increasingly try to obfuscate or disguise their automated traffic to make it appear to originate from a human consumer, evidence of an escalating bot-detection arms race against automated portal access. — *(supporting / secondary / 2025-11-07)*
- Even with an aggregator intermediary, password recovery is not centralized: agents must use 'Forgot Password' on each carrier's own login screen or contact carriers directly for some portals (e.g., biBERK, LightSpeed, Steadily). — *(supporting / primary / 2025 (copyright year only; no specific publish date listed))*
- Major US banks (Wells Fargo, PNC, TD Bank) have actively moved to block third-party aggregators from screen-scraping customer data accessed via customer login credentials, including sending cease-and-desist letters and filing lawsuits, demonstrating institutional hostility to credential-based automated portal access. — *(supporting / secondary / 2025-11-07)*
- Aggregators historically gained portal access by collecting customer usernames and passwords and then impersonating the consumer to log into the institution's portal and copy data - the exact credential-replay model a credential-vault startup would use, which institutions are now actively resisting. — *(central / secondary / 2025-11-07)*
- Institutions object to scraping/automated login on operational grounds because it blocks their servers and interferes with fraud-detection (bot-detection) systems, indicating automated portal access triggers defensive countermeasures. — *(supporting / secondary / 2025-11-07)*
- The industry resolution path has shifted from credential-based scraping toward sanctioned API-based data sharing (e.g., Akoya, Plaid's post-litigation API agreements), often involving paid data access agreements - suggesting credential-scraping aggregators are pressured into licensed API arrangements. — *(supporting / secondary / 2025-11-07)*

- Credential-based aggregators have historically operated without the consumer's full awareness and accumulated large stores of sensitive data with limited safeguarding standards, a pattern that draws regulatory scrutiny. — *(supporting / primary / 2020-02-20)*
